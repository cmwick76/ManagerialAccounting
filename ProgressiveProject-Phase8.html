üîç Tutorial 1: Relevant Costs & Decision Making

Relevant costs are future costs that differ between alternatives. Mastering relevant cost analysis is critical for sound business decisions.

What Makes a Cost Relevant?

Criterion Explanation Example
Future Cost Must occur in the future Materials for new product (relevant)
Past R&D costs (NOT relevant - sunk cost)
Differs Between Alternatives Must be different under each option Direct materials differ (relevant)
Factory rent same regardless (NOT relevant)
‚ö†Ô∏è SUNK COSTS: Money already spent is NEVER relevant! It's gone regardless of what you decide. Don't let sunk costs bias your decision. Example: "We already spent $50K on this project" - that's sunk, ignore it!

Types of Irrelevant Costs

  1. Sunk Costs: Already incurred, can't be changed (past R&D, depreciation on existing equipment)
  2. Future costs that DON'T differ: Same under all alternatives (allocated overhead, executive salaries)
  3. Non-cash items: Depreciation on owned assets (no cash outflow)

Opportunity Costs

Definition: The benefit you give up by choosing one alternative over another.

Example:

You own a warehouse. Two options:

Option A: Use it for production (no rent paid)

Option B: Rent it out for $30,000/year

Opportunity cost of Option A: $30,000 (the rent you give up)

Even though you don't write a check, using the warehouse costs you $30,000 in foregone rent!

üí° OPPORTUNITY COSTS ARE REAL: Just because you don't pay cash doesn't mean there's no cost. Opportunity costs are relevant and should be included in decision analysis!

Avoidable vs. Unavoidable Costs

Cost Type Avoidable? Relevant?
Direct materials for product Yes (if we stop making it) ‚úì Relevant
Product line manager salary Yes (if we drop the line) ‚úì Relevant
Allocated headquarters costs No (HQ still exists) ‚úó NOT Relevant
Lease with 3 years remaining No (contractual obligation) ‚úó NOT Relevant

Relevant Cost Decision Framework

  1. Identify the decision: What choice are we making?
  2. List all costs: What costs are associated with each alternative?
  3. Eliminate sunk costs: Cross out anything already spent
  4. Eliminate costs that don't differ: If same under all options, ignore
  5. Include opportunity costs: What are we giving up?
  6. Compare only relevant costs: Which alternative has lower relevant costs or higher relevant benefits?

Example: Make vs. Buy Decision

Cost Item Make Buy Relevant?
Direct materials $15,000 $0 ‚úì Yes
Direct labor $12,000 $0 ‚úì Yes
Variable overhead $5,000 $0 ‚úì Yes
Purchase price $0 $35,000 ‚úì Yes
Allocated overhead $8,000 $8,000 ‚úó No (doesn't differ)
Equipment depreciation $3,000 $3,000 ‚úó No (sunk cost)
Relevant Costs $32,000 $35,000 Make is cheaper!
‚ö†Ô∏è COMMON MISTAKE: Including allocated overhead or depreciation in relevant cost analysis. These typically DON'T differ between alternatives and should be excluded!

üè≠ Tutorial 2: Make-or-Buy & Special Orders

Common tactical decisions that require relevant cost analysis.

Make-or-Buy Decisions

Question: Should we manufacture a component internally or purchase it from a supplier?

Analysis Steps:

  1. Calculate cost to make: Include ONLY avoidable costs (DM, DL, variable OH, avoidable fixed OH)
  2. Compare to purchase price: Plus any incremental costs (shipping, inspection)
  3. Consider opportunity costs: Could freed-up capacity be used for something else?
  4. Evaluate qualitative factors: Quality, reliability, strategic control

Example: Apex Component Decision

Make internally:

  • Direct materials: $8 per unit
  • Direct labor: $12 per unit
  • Variable overhead: $4 per unit
  • Avoidable fixed overhead: $15,000 total
  • Unavoidable fixed overhead: $10,000 (allocated, continues regardless)

Buy from supplier: $28 per unit for 5,000 units

Analysis:

Make:
  Variable costs: 5,000 √ó ($8+$12+$4) $120,000
  Avoidable fixed $15,000
Total relevant cost to make $135,000
Buy:
  Purchase: 5,000 √ó $28 $140,000
Decision: Make (saves $5,000) ‚úì

Special Order Decisions

Question: Should we accept a one-time order at below-normal price?

Key Considerations:

  • Excess capacity: Do we have unused production capacity?
  • Relevant costs: Only INCREMENTAL costs matter (variable costs + any special costs)
  • Fixed costs: Usually irrelevant (already being paid)
  • Will it affect regular sales? Could it cannibalize normal business?

Example: Special Order at $70/unit

Normal selling price: $95

Current production: 10,000 units (capacity: 15,000)

Variable cost per unit: $57

Special order: 3,000 units at $70

Analysis:

Revenue from special order: 3,000 √ó $70 $210,000
Incremental costs: 3,000 √ó $57 ($171,000)
Incremental profit $39,000

Decision: Accept! Adds $39,000 to profit (we have excess capacity)

üí° PRICING FLOOR: The absolute minimum price for a special order is the variable cost per unit. Anything above variable cost contributes to covering fixed costs and profit. BUT: only works with excess capacity!
‚ö†Ô∏è EXCESS CAPACITY REQUIRED: Special orders only make sense if you have unused capacity. If at full capacity, accepting a special order means giving up regular sales‚Äîthen you must compare to the opportunity cost!

‚è±Ô∏è Tutorial 3: Time Value of Money Fundamentals

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because you can invest it and earn returns. This concept drives all capital budgeting decisions.

Present Value vs. Future Value

Concept Question Formula
Future Value (FV) What will money grow to? FV = PV √ó (1 + r)‚Åø
Present Value (PV) What is future money worth today? PV = FV √∑ (1 + r)‚Åø

Where:

  • r = discount rate (required return, cost of capital)
  • n = number of periods (years)

Example: Future Value

Invest $10,000 today at 8% for 5 years. What will you have?

FV = $10,000 √ó (1.08)‚Åµ = $10,000 √ó 1.469 = $14,693

Example: Present Value

You'll receive $20,000 in 3 years. Worth today at 10% discount rate?

PV = $20,000 √∑ (1.10)¬≥ = $20,000 √∑ 1.331 = $15,026

Interpretation: $20,000 in 3 years = $15,026 today (at 10%)

üí∞ Tutorial 4: NPV & IRR Analysis

Net Present Value and Internal Rate of Return are the two most powerful capital budgeting methods.

Net Present Value (NPV)

Definition: The present value of all cash inflows minus the present value of all cash outflows.

NPV Formula:
NPV = PV(Cash Inflows) - Initial Investment
Or: NPV = -Initial + CF‚ÇÅ/(1+r) + CF‚ÇÇ/(1+r)¬≤ + ... + CF‚Çô/(1+r)‚Åø

NPV Decision Rule:

NPV Result Decision Meaning
NPV > 0 Accept Project returns MORE than required rate
NPV = 0 Indifferent Project returns EXACTLY the required rate
NPV < 0 Reject Project returns LESS than required rate

Example: Equipment Investment

Initial cost: $100,000

Annual cash inflows: $30,000 for 5 years

Discount rate: 10%

Excel Formula:

=NPV(0.10, 30000, 30000, 30000, 30000, 30000) - 100000

OR using range:

=NPV(0.10, B2:B6) - A1

Result: NPV = $13,724

Decision: Accept! NPV > 0 means project adds value.

‚ö†Ô∏è EXCEL NPV GOTCHA: Excel's NPV function assumes cash flows occur at END of each period. The initial investment (Year 0) should be SUBTRACTED separately, not included in the NPV range!

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Definition: The discount rate that makes NPV equal to zero.

IRR Concept:
Find the rate (r) where: 0 = -Initial Investment + CF‚ÇÅ/(1+r) + CF‚ÇÇ/(1+r)¬≤ + ...

IRR Decision Rule:

IRR vs. Required Return Decision Meaning
IRR > Required Return Accept Project earns more than minimum
IRR = Required Return Indifferent Project earns exactly the minimum
IRR < Required Return Reject Project earns less than minimum

Same Example with IRR:

Cash flows: -$100,000 (Year 0), $30,000 (Years 1-5)

Excel Formula:

=IRR(A1:A6)

(Where A1 = -100000, A2:A6 = 30000 each)

Result: IRR = 15.24%

Decision: Accept! 15.24% > 10% required return.

üí° NPV vs. IRR: Both usually give same accept/reject decision. NPV is theoretically superior (shows dollar value added). IRR is intuitive (shows percentage return). Use both!

Comparing Multiple Projects

Project Initial Cost NPV @ 10% IRR Ranking
Project A $50,000 $8,500 18% 2nd (by NPV)
Project B $100,000 $13,724 15.24% 1st (by NPV) ‚úì
Project C $75,000 $5,200 12% 3rd

When choosing ONE project: Pick highest NPV (Project B). It adds most value!

‚ö†Ô∏è IRR CAN MISLEAD: Project A has highest IRR (18%) but Project B has highest NPV ($13,724). Always choose based on NPV when there's a conflict‚Äîit shows actual value created!

üìä Tutorial 5: Payback Period & Accounting Rate of Return

Simpler capital budgeting methods that don't use time value of money.

Payback Period

Definition: How long until we recover our initial investment?

Payback Period Formula (even cash flows):
Payback = Initial Investment √∑ Annual Cash Flow

Example: Even Cash Flows

Initial investment: $100,000

Annual cash inflow: $30,000

Payback = $100,000 √∑ $30,000 = 3.33 years

Interpretation: We'll recover our investment in 3 years and 4 months.

Payback with Uneven Cash Flows

Accumulate cash flows year by year until investment is recovered:

Year Cash Flow Cumulative
0 ($100,000) ($100,000)
1 $25,000 ($75,000)
2 $35,000 ($40,000)
3 $40,000 $0
Payback = 3.0 years (cumulative reaches zero)

Payback Decision Rule

Accept if: Payback period < Company's maximum acceptable payback

Example: Company requires payback within 4 years. Project with 3.33-year payback ‚Üí Accept!

‚ö†Ô∏è PAYBACK LIMITATIONS:
  • Ignores time value of money
  • Ignores cash flows AFTER payback
  • No consideration of profitability
  • Useful for: Risk assessment, liquidity concerns, quick screening

Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

Definition: Average annual accounting profit as a percentage of investment.

ARR Formula:
ARR = Average Annual Income √∑ Average Investment
Where: Average Investment = (Initial Investment + Salvage Value) √∑ 2

Example:

Initial investment: $100,000

Salvage value after 5 years: $10,000

Annual revenues: $60,000

Annual cash expenses: $30,000

Annual depreciation: $18,000 [($100K - $10K) √∑ 5]

Step 1: Average Annual Income

Revenues: $60,000
Cash expenses: ($30,000)
Depreciation: ($18,000)
Net income: $12,000

Step 2: Average Investment

($100,000 + $10,000) √∑ 2 = $55,000

Step 3: ARR

ARR = $12,000 √∑ $55,000 = 21.8%

ARR Decision Rule

Accept if: ARR > Company's minimum required ARR

Example: Company requires 15% ARR. Project with 21.8% ‚Üí Accept!

üí° ARR vs. IRR: Both are percentages, but ARR uses accounting income (not cash flows) and ignores time value. IRR is superior for capital budgeting. ARR is useful for quick comparisons to ROI targets.

Method Comparison Summary

Method Pros Cons Best For
NPV Time value, shows $ value added Less intuitive Primary decision tool
IRR Time value, intuitive % Can mislead on size Quick assessment
Payback Simple, shows liquidity Ignores TVM & later CFs Risk screening
ARR Simple, matches ROI Ignores TVM, uses income Quick comparison

Best Practice: Use NPV as primary method, supplement with IRR, payback, and qualitative factors.

Building a Decision Matrix

A decision matrix evaluates alternatives across multiple criteria.

  1. List alternatives (rows): Projects A, B, C
  2. List criteria (columns): NPV, IRR, Payback, Risk, Strategic Fit
  3. Weight criteria: Assign importance (total = 100%)
  4. Score each alternative: Rate 1-10 on each criterion
  5. Calculate weighted scores: Score √ó Weight
  6. Sum total scores: Highest = best choice

Qualitative Factors

Never rely on numbers alone! Consider:

Factor Category Questions to Ask
Strategic Alignment Does this support our long-term strategy? Core competencies?
Risk What could go wrong? How certain are cash flow estimates?
Quality Will this improve product/service quality? Customer satisfaction?
Employee Impact Effects on morale, skills needed, training required?
Competitive Advantage Differentiation? Market position? Barriers to entry?
Flexibility Reversible? Options to expand/contract? Adaptability?
Environmental/Social Sustainability? Community impact? Regulatory compliance?

Sensitivity Analysis

Test how changes in assumptions affect the decision.

  1. Identify key variables: Sales volume, price, costs, discount rate
  2. Create scenarios: Best case, most likely, worst case
  3. Recalculate NPV/IRR: For each scenario
  4. Assess risk: How sensitive is the decision?

Example: NPV Sensitivity to Sales Volume

Scenario Sales Volume NPV Decision
Best Case 15,000 units $45,000 Accept
Most Likely 12,000 units $13,724 Accept
Worst Case 9,000 units ($8,500) Reject!

Insight: Project is risky! NPV turns negative if sales fall 25%. Need contingency plan.

Excel Decision Model Template

Build a comprehensive decision model:

  1. Assumptions tab: All inputs (sales, costs, discount rate)
  2. Cash flow tab: Project cash flows by year
  3. Analysis tab: NPV, IRR, payback, ARR calculations
  4. Sensitivity tab: Data tables with scenario analysis
  5. Dashboard tab: Summary with recommendations
üí° BEST PRACTICE: Use Excel's Data Table feature for sensitivity analysis. Change one or two variables, see how NPV changes. Creates instant "what-if" analysis!

Final Decision Framework

  1. Quantitative screening: Calculate NPV, IRR, payback, ARR
  2. Eliminate poor options: Reject projects with NPV < 0
  3. Rank remaining options: By NPV (highest value)
  4. Evaluate qualitatively: Strategic fit, risk, other factors
  5. Test sensitivity: How robust is the decision?
  6. Make recommendation: Combine all factors
  7. Document rationale: Show your work!
‚ö†Ô∏è REMEMBER: The best quantitative analysis won't help if your assumptions are wrong. Garbage in, garbage out! Spend time getting good estimates. When in doubt, be conservative.

Result: IRR = 15.24%

Decision: Accept! 15.24% > 10% required return.

üí° NPV vs. IRR: Both usually give same accept/reject decision. NPV is theoretically superior (shows dollar value added). IRR is intuitive (shows percentage return). Use both!

Comparing Multiple Projects

Project Initial Cost NPV @ 10% IRR Ranking
Project A $50,000 $8,500 18% 2nd (by NPV)
Project B $100,000 $13,724 15.24% 1st (by NPV) ‚úì
Project C $75,000 $5,200 12% 3rd

When choosing ONE project: Pick highest NPV (Project B). It adds most value!

‚ö†Ô∏è IRR CAN MISLEAD: Project A has highest IRR (18%) but Project B has highest NPV ($13,724). Always choose based on NPV when there's a conflict‚Äîit shows actual value created!

üìä Tutorial 5: Payback Period & Accounting Rate of Return

Simpler capital budgeting methods that don't use time value of money.

Payback Period

Definition: How long until we recover our initial investment?

Payback Period Formula (even cash flows):
Payback = Initial Investment √∑ Annual Cash Flow

Example: Even Cash Flows

Initial investment: $100,000

Annual cash inflow: $30,000

Payback = $100,000 √∑ $30,000 = 3.33 years

Interpretation: We'll recover our investment in 3 years and 4 months.

Payback with Uneven Cash Flows

Accumulate cash flows year by year until investment is recovered:

Year Cash Flow Cumulative
0 ($100,000) ($100,000)
1 $25,000 ($75,000)
2 $35,000 ($40,000)
3 $40,000 $0
Payback = 3.0 years (cumulative reaches zero)

Payback Decision Rule

Accept if: Payback period < Company's maximum acceptable payback

Example: Company requires payback within 4 years. Project with 3.33-year payback ‚Üí Accept!

‚ö†Ô∏è PAYBACK LIMITATIONS:
  • Ignores time value of money
  • Ignores cash flows AFTER payback
  • No consideration of profitability
  • Useful for: Risk assessment, liquidity concerns, quick screening

Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

Definition: Average annual accounting profit as a percentage of investment.

ARR Formula:
ARR = Average Annual Income √∑ Average Investment
Where: Average Investment = (Initial Investment + Salvage Value) √∑ 2

Example:

Initial investment: $100,000

Salvage value after 5 years: $10,000

Annual revenues: $60,000

Annual cash expenses: $30,000

Annual depreciation: $18,000 [($100K - $10K) √∑ 5]

Step 1: Average Annual Income

Revenues: $60,000
Cash expenses: ($30,000)
Depreciation: ($18,000)
Net income: $12,000

Step 2: Average Investment

($100,000 + $10,000) √∑ 2 = $55,000

Step 3: ARR

ARR = $12,000 √∑ $55,000 = 21.8%

ARR Decision Rule

Accept if: ARR > Company's minimum required ARR

Example: Company requires 15% ARR. Project with 21.8% ‚Üí Accept!

üí° ARR vs. IRR: Both are percentages, but ARR uses accounting income (not cash flows) and ignores time value. IRR is superior for capital budgeting. ARR is useful for quick comparisons to ROI targets.

Method Comparison Summary

Method Pros Cons Best For
NPV Time value, shows $ value added Less intuitive Primary decision tool
IRR Time value, intuitive % Can mislead on size Quick assessment
Payback Simple, shows liquidity Ignores TVM & later CFs Risk screening
ARR Simple, matches ROI Ignores TVM, uses income Quick comparison

Best Practice: Use NPV as primary method, supplement with IRR, payback, and qualitative factors.

‚ö° Tutorial 6: Operating Leverage & Risk Analysis

Operating leverage measures how sensitive profit is to changes in sales. High operating leverage means small sales changes cause large profit swings - both good and bad!

What is Operating Leverage?

Operating leverage comes from fixed costs. The more fixed costs relative to variable costs, the higher the leverage:

  • High fixed, low variable: High operating leverage (e.g., airlines, utilities)
  • Low fixed, high variable: Low operating leverage (e.g., consulting, retail)

Degree of Operating Leverage (DOL)

DOL Formula:
DOL = Contribution Margin √∑ Net Operating Income
Interpretation: If sales increase by 10%, profit increases by (10% √ó DOL)

Example Calculation:

Contribution Margin: $190,000

Net Operating Income: $70,000

DOL = $190,000 √∑ $70,000 = 2.71

Interpretation: If sales increase 10%, profit increases 27.1% (10% √ó 2.71)

How DOL Works - The Multiplier Effect

Sales Change Profit Change (DOL = 2.71) New Profit
+10% increase +27.1% increase $70,000 √ó 1.271 = $88,970
+20% increase +54.2% increase $70,000 √ó 1.542 = $107,940
-10% decrease -27.1% decrease $70,000 √ó 0.729 = $51,030
-20% decrease -54.2% decrease $70,000 √ó 0.458 = $32,060
üí° THE DOUBLE-EDGED SWORD: High DOL magnifies BOTH gains and losses. Good when sales growing (profit skyrockets). Dangerous when sales declining (profit plummets). Low DOL = more stable but less upside.

Operating Leverage and Risk

DOL reveals business risk:

DOL Level Risk Profile Characteristics
DOL > 3 High Risk Profits very sensitive to sales, high fixed costs, near break-even
DOL 1.5-3 Moderate Risk Balanced cost structure, reasonable profit cushion
DOL < 1.5 Low Risk Mostly variable costs, stable profits, well above break-even

DOL Changes with Sales Level

Important: DOL is NOT constant! It changes as you move away from current sales:

  • Near break-even: DOL is very high (small profit base)
  • Far above break-even: DOL decreases (large profit cushion)
  • At break-even: DOL is undefined (division by zero)

DOL at Different Sales Levels:

Units CM Profit DOL
3,500 $133,000 $13,000 10.23
5,000 $190,000 $70,000 2.71
7,000 $266,000 $146,000 1.82

Notice: As volume increases (farther from break-even), DOL decreases

Strategic Implications

Understanding operating leverage guides strategic decisions:

When to Increase Operating Leverage (More Fixed, Less Variable):

  • Confident about strong, growing sales
  • Want to maximize profit in good times
  • Automate production (high fixed equipment, lower labor)
  • Example: Replace hourly workers with automated machines

When to Decrease Operating Leverage (Less Fixed, More Variable):

  • Uncertain economic conditions
  • Want stability over maximum profit
  • Use temporary/contract labor instead of salaried employees
  • Outsource instead of owning equipment
‚ö†Ô∏è LEVERAGE TRAP: Companies with high operating leverage face huge pressure in downturns. They can't easily reduce fixed costs (rent, salaries) but revenues drop. This is why airlines and hotels struggle in recessions - high DOL combined with falling demand!

Financial Leverage vs. Operating Leverage

Don't confuse these:

Type Source Impact
Operating Leverage Fixed operating costs Magnifies effect of sales on operating profit
Financial Leverage Fixed financing costs (debt interest) Magnifies effect of operating profit on net income

Combined leverage (both together) creates even more volatility!
