üìä Tutorial 1: Creating Your First Pivot Table

Pivot tables are one of Excel's most powerful features for analyzing and summarizing large datasets. Instead of writing complex formulas, you can drag and drop fields to instantly create summaries, comparisons, and insights.

What is a Pivot Table?

A pivot table takes your raw data (like 15 manufacturing jobs with details) and lets you quickly answer questions like:

  • What's the total cost by department?
  • How many jobs did we complete each month?
  • Which customer had the highest total costs?
  • What percentage of total costs does each department represent?

Step-by-Step: Creating a Pivot Table

  1. Select your data - Click anywhere in your job data table (Excel will auto-detect the range)
  2. Go to Insert tab in the ribbon
  3. Click "PivotTable" button (on the left side)
  4. Choose where to place it:
    • New Worksheet (Recommended): Creates a clean pivot table on a new tab
    • Existing Worksheet: Places it on current sheet (specify location)
  5. Click OK
  6. You'll see: Blank pivot table area on left, PivotTable Fields pane on right

Understanding the PivotTable Fields Pane

This pane has two sections:

Section What It Does Example for Job Costing
Field List (Top) Shows all available columns from your data Job Number, Department, Direct Materials, Total Job Cost, etc.
Rows Categories going down (vertical) Department names (Fabrication, Assembly, Finishing)
Columns Categories going across (horizontal) Month Completed (January, February, March...)
Values Numbers to calculate (SUM, COUNT, AVERAGE) Total Job Cost, Direct Labor, Machine Hours
Filters Optional filters for the whole table Status (Completed/In Progress), Customer Name
üí° PRO TIP: Always start with your data formatted as an Excel Table (Ctrl+T). This ensures your pivot table updates automatically when you add new jobs to your data!

Example: Total Costs by Department

To answer "What's the total cost for each department?"

1. Drag Department to ROWS

2. Drag Total Job Cost to VALUES

Result: A simple table showing Fabrication $X, Assembly $Y, Finishing $Z

‚ö†Ô∏è COMMON MISTAKE: If your pivot table shows "Count of Total Job Cost" instead of "Sum of Total Job Cost", it means Excel thinks your cost column contains text. Check your source data - there might be a cell with text instead of a number (like "$1,234" with the dollar sign typed in).

‚ûï Tutorial 2: Adding and Arranging Fields

The power of pivot tables comes from how easily you can rearrange fields to answer different questions. Let's learn how to add, move, and organize fields.

Adding Fields to Your Pivot Table

  1. Find the field in the Field List (top of pane)
  2. Drag it to one of the four areas (Rows, Columns, Values, Filters)
  3. Or check the box next to the field name - Excel auto-places it:
    • Text fields ‚Üí go to Rows
    • Number fields ‚Üí go to Values (and SUM automatically)
    • Date fields ‚Üí go to Rows (and group by month automatically)

Moving Fields Around

You can drag fields between areas to reorganize your analysis:

Example: Switching Rows and Columns

Before: Rows = Department, Columns = (none)

Shows: List of departments with totals

After: Rows = Month, Columns = Department

Shows: Grid with months down the left, departments across the top

Task 1: Costs by Department with % of Total

  1. Add Department to ROWS
  2. Add Total Job Cost to VALUES (should show Sum of Total Job Cost)
  3. Add Total Job Cost AGAIN to VALUES (drag it a second time)
  4. Click the second "Sum of Total Job Cost" in VALUES area
  5. Choose "Value Field Settings"
  6. Click "Show Values As" tab
  7. Select "% of Grand Total"
  8. Click OK

Your pivot table should now show:

Department | Sum of Total Job Cost | % of Total
Fabrication | $285,000 | 38%
Assembly | $310,000 | 41%
Finishing | $155,000 | 21%
Grand Total | $750,000 | 100%

Task 2: Monthly Job Volume and Costs

  1. Start fresh - Create a new pivot table from your job data
  2. Add Month Completed to ROWS
  3. Add Job Number to VALUES (will show "Count of Job Number" = # of jobs)
  4. Add Total Job Cost to VALUES (will show "Sum of Total Job Cost")
  5. Add Direct Materials to VALUES
  6. Add Direct Labor Cost to VALUES
üí° PRO TIP: You can rename fields in the pivot table! Right-click on "Sum of Total Job Cost" in your pivot table and type a cleaner name like "Total Cost" or "Job Cost".

Removing Fields

To remove a field, simply drag it out of the area boxes back to the field list, or uncheck its box.

‚ö†Ô∏è IMPORTANT: Don't delete rows or columns from your pivot table manually! Use the field pane to add/remove data. Manually deleting can break the pivot table.

üî¢ Tutorial 3: Understanding Aggregations (SUM, COUNT, AVERAGE)

When you add a field to VALUES, Excel performs a calculation. By default it SUMs numbers and COUNTs text, but you can change this.

Common Aggregation Functions

Function What It Does When to Use Example
Sum Adds all values Total costs, total hours, total materials Sum of Total Job Cost = $750,000
Count Counts how many items Number of jobs, number of customers Count of Job Number = 15 jobs
Average Mean (sum √∑ count) Average job cost, average labor hours Average of Total Job Cost = $50,000
Max Highest value Largest job cost, most hours Max of Total Job Cost = $85,000
Min Lowest value Smallest job cost, fewest hours Min of Total Job Cost = $12,000

Changing the Aggregation Function

  1. Click the field in the VALUES area (e.g., "Sum of Total Job Cost")
  2. Choose "Value Field Settings"
  3. Select the function you want from the list
  4. Optional: Change the "Custom Name" to something clearer
  5. Click OK

Example: Finding Average Job Cost by Department

1. Department in ROWS

2. Total Job Cost in VALUES

3. Click "Sum of Total Job Cost" ‚Üí Value Field Settings

4. Change to "Average"

5. Custom Name: "Avg Job Cost"

Result: Shows Fabrication avg $47,500, Assembly avg $51,667, etc.

Show Values As (Advanced Calculations)

Beyond basic aggregations, you can show values as percentages, differences, running totals, and more.

Show Values As What It Shows Use Case
% of Grand Total Each value as % of overall total What % of costs is each department?
% of Column Total Each value as % of its column Within each month, what % is each department?
% of Row Total Each value as % of its row For each department, what % is each month?
Running Total Cumulative sum Year-to-date cumulative costs by month
Difference From Variance from a base value Cost difference vs. January
üí° MULTI-VALUE ANALYSIS: You can add the same field multiple times to VALUES! Add Total Job Cost three times, then set one to SUM, one to AVERAGE, and one to COUNT. Instant comprehensive analysis!
// Quick calculation examples Department in ROWS + Total Cost in VALUES = Total by department Customer in ROWS + Job Number in VALUES = Count of jobs per customer Month in ROWS + Direct Labor in VALUES = Monthly labor costs Department in ROWS + Machine Hours (Average) = Avg hours per dept

üé® Tutorial 4: Professional Pivot Table Formatting

Raw pivot tables are functional but not pretty. Professional formatting makes your analysis easy to read and presentation-ready.

Applying a Pivot Table Style

  1. Click anywhere in your pivot table
  2. PivotTable Design tab appears in ribbon
  3. Browse the PivotTable Styles gallery
  4. Hover to preview, click to apply
  5. Recommended: Choose a style with blue headers to match Apex branding
üí° CUSTOM COLORS: In PivotTable Design tab, click the dropdown below the styles gallery. Choose "New PivotTable Style" to create one using Apex brand colors (#0033A0 for headers, #FFCB1F for accents).

Essential Formatting Options

Option Location Purpose
Banded Rows Design tab ‚Üí Checkboxes Alternating row colors for easier reading
Banded Columns Design tab ‚Üí Checkboxes Alternating column colors
Row/Column Headers Design tab ‚Üí Checkboxes Bold formatting for headers
Grand Totals Design tab ‚Üí Grand Totals button Show/hide totals for rows and columns
Subtotals Design tab ‚Üí Subtotals button Show totals for grouped items
Report Layout Design tab ‚Üí Report Layout button Compact, Outline, or Tabular form

Number Formatting in Pivot Tables

  1. Right-click any value in your pivot table
  2. Choose "Value Field Settings"
  3. Click "Number Format" button at bottom
  4. Select Currency (for dollar amounts) or Number (for ratios/counts)
  5. Set decimal places:
    • 0 decimals for dollar amounts ($750,000)
    • 1-2 decimals for percentages (38.5%)
    • 0 decimals for counts (15 jobs)
  6. Click OK twice

Before Formatting:

Fabrication | 285000
Assembly | 310000

After Formatting:

Department | Total Cost
Fabrication | $285,000
Assembly | $310,000

üìà Tutorial 5: Creating Pivot Charts

Pivot charts are dynamic charts linked to your pivot table. When you change the pivot table, the chart updates automatically!

Creating a Pivot Chart

  1. Click anywhere in your pivot table
  2. Go to PivotTable Analyze tab (or Insert tab)
  3. Click "PivotChart" button
  4. Choose chart type:
    • Clustered Column: Best for comparing categories (use for Task 4!)
    • Line: Best for trends over time
    • Pie: Best for showing parts of a whole (one series only)
    • Bar: Like column but horizontal
  5. Click OK

Task 4: Clustered Column Pivot Chart

Create a chart showing Total Job Cost by Department:

  1. Use your pivot table from Task 1 (Department in ROWS, Total Job Cost in VALUES)
  2. Click in the pivot table
  3. PivotTable Analyze ‚Üí PivotChart
  4. Select "Clustered Column"
  5. Click OK
  6. Chart appears! You should see three bars (Fabrication, Assembly, Finishing)

Formatting Your Pivot Chart

When you click the chart, two new tabs appear: Chart Design and Format

Adding/Editing Chart Title

  1. Click the chart title (says "Total" by default)
  2. Type your title: "Total Job Cost by Department"
  3. Or use Chart Design ‚Üí Add Chart Element ‚Üí Chart Title ‚Üí Above Chart

Changing Colors

  1. Chart Design tab ‚Üí Change Colors dropdown
  2. Choose a color scheme - Blue theme matches Apex branding
  3. Or manually: Click a bar ‚Üí Format tab ‚Üí Shape Fill ‚Üí choose color

Adding Data Labels

  1. Chart Design tab ‚Üí Add Chart Element
  2. Data Labels ‚Üí Outside End
  3. Shows actual values ($285,000, etc.) on each bar

Your clustered column chart should show:

[Chart Title: "Total Job Cost by Department"]
Three vertical bars of different heights
X-axis: Fabrication, Assembly, Finishing
Y-axis: Dollar amounts
Data labels showing $285,000, $310,000, $155,000
üí° CHART FILTERS: Pivot charts have filter buttons built-in! Click the filter icon on the chart to show/hide specific departments, months, or other fields without changing the underlying pivot table.

Advanced: Multi-Series Charts

To show Department by Month (multiple series):

  1. Modify your pivot table: Department in ROWS, Month in COLUMNS, Total Cost in VALUES
  2. Create pivot chart ‚Üí Clustered Column
  3. Result: Grouped bars showing each department's costs across all months
‚ö†Ô∏è DON'T COPY/PASTE: Create pivot charts using the PivotChart button, not by creating a regular chart from pivot table data. Pivot charts stay linked to your pivot table for automatic updates!

üå°Ô∏è Tutorial 6: Conditional Formatting in Pivot Tables

Conditional formatting applies color scales, data bars, or icons to highlight patterns and outliers in your pivot table.

Applying Conditional Formatting to Task 3

Remember Task 3 created a Department by Month matrix. Now let's add color to show which cells have the highest/lowest costs:

  1. Click in your pivot table (the one with Department √ó Month)
  2. Select the VALUES area (click any cost number, then Ctrl+A selects all values)
  3. Home tab ‚Üí Conditional Formatting dropdown
  4. Choose an option:
    • Color Scales: Gradient from low (red) to high (green)
    • Data Bars: Bars inside cells showing relative values
    • Icon Sets: Arrows, traffic lights, or rating symbols
  5. Click your choice

Recommended: Color Scale for Task 3

  1. Select values in your pivot table
  2. Conditional Formatting ‚Üí Color Scales
  3. Choose "Green-Yellow-Red Color Scale"
    • Green = Highest costs (most activity)
    • Yellow = Medium costs
    • Red = Lowest costs (least activity)

Your formatted matrix will look like:

Department √ó Month grid with cells colored:
‚ñ† Dark green cells = $80,000+ (busy months)
‚ñ† Yellow cells = $40,000-$80,000 (moderate)
‚ñ† Red cells = Under $40,000 (slow months)
At a glance, you see which department-month combinations had highest costs!

Data Bars Option

Data bars show relative size visually:

  1. Select pivot table values
  2. Conditional Formatting ‚Üí Data Bars
  3. Choose "Blue Data Bar" (matches Apex brand)
  4. Each cell shows a blue bar proportional to its value
  5. Numbers still visible, but bars make comparison instant

Custom Rules (Advanced)

For more control, create custom rules:

  1. Conditional Formatting ‚Üí New Rule
  2. Choose rule type (e.g., "Format cells that contain")
  3. Set conditions: Greater than $75,000 ‚Üí Fill green
  4. Between $40,000 and $75,000 ‚Üí Fill yellow
  5. Less than $40,000 ‚Üí Fill red
üí° MANAGING RULES: Go to Conditional Formatting ‚Üí Manage Rules to edit or delete rules. If your formatting looks wrong, you might have multiple conflicting rules applied.

Icon Sets for Trends

Use icon sets to show performance levels:

  1. Select values
  2. Conditional Formatting ‚Üí Icon Sets
  3. Choose "3 Traffic Lights"
    • üü¢ Green = Top 33% (high costs)
    • üü° Yellow = Middle 33%
    • üî¥ Red = Bottom 33% (low costs)
‚ö†Ô∏è READABILITY: Don't over-format! Choose ONE type of conditional formatting per pivot table. Combining color scales + data bars + icons makes it confusing, not clearer.

Clearing Conditional Formatting

To start over:

  • Select pivot table values
  • Conditional Formatting ‚Üí Clear Rules ‚Üí Clear Rules from Selected Cells
