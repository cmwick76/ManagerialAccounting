üè≠ Tutorial 2: Operating Budgets

Operating budgets detail the income-generating activities: selling products and the costs to produce them.

1. Sales Budget - The Starting Point

Everything flows from expected sales. The sales budget drives all other budgets.

Sales Budget Formula:

Expected Unit Sales √ó Selling Price per Unit = Total Sales Revenue

Example: Apex Q1 Sales Budget

January February March Quarter
Expected unit sales 10,000 12,000 15,000 37,000
Selling price per unit $95 $95 $95 $95
Total sales revenue $950,000 $1,140,000 $1,425,000 $3,515,000

2. Production Budget

How many units must we produce to meet sales AND maintain desired inventory?

Production Budget Formula:

Units needed for sales
+ Desired ending inventory
= Total needs
- Beginning inventory
= Units to produce

Example: Apex Production Budget (January)

Expected sales: 10,000 units

Desired ending inventory: 2,400 units (20% of next month's sales)

Beginning inventory: 2,000 units

10,000 sales + 2,400 ending - 2,000 beginning = 10,400 units to produce

üí° INVENTORY POLICY: Common policy is ending inventory = X% of next month's sales. This buffers against stockouts while avoiding excess inventory costs. Typical range: 10-30%.

3. Direct Materials Budget

How much raw material to purchase based on production needs?

Materials Purchases Formula:

Materials needed for production (units √ó lbs per unit)
+ Desired ending materials inventory
= Total materials needed
- Beginning materials inventory
= Materials to purchase

Materials to purchase √ó Cost per lb = Purchase cost

Example: Direct Materials Budget

Units to produce: 10,400

Materials per unit: 3 lbs

Materials needed: 10,400 √ó 3 = 31,200 lbs

+ Ending inventory: 7,200 lbs (20% of next month)

= Total needs: 38,400 lbs

- Beginning inventory: 6,240 lbs

= 32,160 lbs to purchase

At $12 per lb: $385,920 purchase cost

4. Direct Labor Budget

Labor hours and cost needed for production.

Direct Labor Budget Formula:

Units to produce √ó Labor hours per unit = Total labor hours
Total labor hours √ó Wage rate per hour = Total labor cost

Example:

10,400 units √ó 0.5 hours/unit = 5,200 hours

5,200 hours √ó $18/hour = $93,600 labor cost

5. Manufacturing Overhead Budget

Both variable and fixed overhead costs.

Manufacturing Overhead Formula:

Variable OH = Production hours √ó Variable OH rate per hour
+ Fixed OH = Monthly fixed amount
= Total manufacturing overhead
- Depreciation (non-cash)
= Cash disbursement for overhead

‚ö†Ô∏è NON-CASH ITEMS: Depreciation is an expense on income statement but doesn't require cash payment. Subtract it when calculating cash disbursements for overhead!

6. Selling & Administrative Expense Budget

Period costs not tied to production volume.

Expense Type Variable or Fixed Example
Variable S&A Based on sales volume Sales commissions, shipping
Fixed S&A Constant each month Salaries, rent, advertising

Operating Budget Flow

Notice how each budget uses information from the previous budget:

Sales Budget ‚Üì (units) Production Budget ‚Üì (units to produce) ‚îú‚Üí Direct Materials Budget ‚îú‚Üí Direct Labor Budget ‚îî‚Üí Manufacturing Overhead Budget ‚Üì (all costs flow to) COGS Budget & Income Statement

üí∞ Tutorial 3: Cash Budget - The Critical Component

The cash budget is often the most important budget because it reveals whether the company will have enough cash to operate, or if borrowing will be needed.

Cash Budget Structure

Cash Budget Format:

Beginning cash balance
+ Cash receipts
= Cash available
- Cash disbursements
= Excess or (deficiency)
+ Borrowing or - Repayments
= Ending cash balance

1. Cash Receipts

When will we actually collect cash from sales?

Collection Pattern Example:

Apex collects:

  • 70% in month of sale
  • 28% in month following sale
  • 2% uncollectible (bad debts)

January Collections:

From January sales ($950,000): $950,000 √ó 70% = $665,000

From December sales ($900,000): $900,000 √ó 28% = $252,000

Total January receipts: $917,000

üí° TIMING IS EVERYTHING: Sales ‚â† Cash receipts! If you sell $100k in January but only collect $70k, you have $30k in accounts receivable. The cash budget tracks actual cash, not accrual accounting!

2. Cash Disbursements

When will we actually pay cash for expenses?

Materials Purchases:

Payment Pattern Example:

Apex pays suppliers:

  • 50% in month of purchase
  • 50% in month following purchase

January Payments:

From January purchases ($385,920): $385,920 √ó 50% = $192,960

From December purchases ($375,000): $375,000 √ó 50% = $187,500

Total January payments: $380,460

Other Disbursements:

  • Direct labor: Usually paid in month incurred
  • Manufacturing overhead: Paid monthly (minus depreciation)
  • S&A expenses: Paid monthly (minus depreciation)
  • Capital expenditures: Equipment purchases
  • Dividends: If declared
  • Income taxes: Quarterly payments

3. Financing Section

Managing the minimum cash balance requirement.

Example: Apex Minimum Cash Balance = $50,000

Beginning cash $65,000
Add: Cash receipts 917,000
Cash available 982,000
Less: Cash disbursements (945,000)
Excess (or deficiency) 37,000
Minimum cash balance required (50,000)
Cash needed (shortfall) (13,000)
Borrowing (rounded to $1,000s) 13,000
Ending cash balance $50,000

Borrowing and Repayment

Key rules for the financing section:

  • Borrow: When excess cash < minimum required
  • Repay: When excess cash > minimum required (after meeting minimum)
  • Interest: Paid on borrowed amounts (calculate based on loan terms)
  • Round borrowing: Usually to nearest $1,000
  • Repay in order borrowed: First in, first out
‚ö†Ô∏è INTEREST CALCULATION: Interest is typically calculated on the outstanding loan balance each month. If you borrow $10,000 at 12% annual (1% monthly), you owe $100 interest the first month. This $100 is a cash disbursement in the NEXT month!

Why Cash Budget Matters Most

Companies can be profitable but still fail due to cash problems:

  • Growing companies: May need cash for inventory/receivables before collecting from customers
  • Seasonal businesses: Build inventory before high season, collect after
  • Capital expenditures: Large equipment purchases require cash planning
  • Debt payments: Principal and interest due regardless of profitability
üí° CASH IS KING: "Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, cash is reality!" You can't pay bills with accrual-basis profit. The cash budget shows whether you'll have actual dollars in the bank!

üîó Tutorial 4: Linking Worksheets in Excel

Master budgets require linking data across multiple worksheets. Changes in one budget automatically update dependent budgets.

Why Link Worksheets?

  • Accuracy: No manual recopying of data between sheets
  • Efficiency: Update one number, all dependent budgets recalculate
  • What-if analysis: Change assumptions and see impact throughout
  • Error reduction: Formulas prevent transcription errors

Basic Worksheet Reference Syntax

// Reference a cell on another worksheet =SheetName!CellAddress // Examples: ='Sales Budget'!B10 // Space in name requires quotes =Production!C15 // No space, no quotes needed ='Cash Budget'!$D$20 // Absolute reference

Step-by-Step: Creating Worksheet Links

  1. Start typing formula in destination cell (e.g., =)
  2. Click the source sheet tab (Excel adds sheet name)
  3. Click the source cell (Excel adds cell address)
  4. Press Enter
  5. Excel creates: ='Source Sheet'!A1

Example: Linking Production Budget to Sales Budget

Goal: Use January sales (from Sales Budget) in Production Budget

In Production Budget sheet, cell B8:

='Sales Budget'!B7

This pulls January unit sales from Sales Budget row 7, column B

Common Linking Patterns in Master Budget

From (Source) To (Destination) What's Linked
Sales Budget Production Budget Unit sales by month
Production Budget DM Budget Units to produce
Production Budget DL Budget Units to produce
Sales Budget Cash Budget (Receipts) Sales revenue by month
DM Budget Cash Budget (Payments) Purchases by month
All budgets Income Statement Revenue and expenses
Cash Budget Balance Sheet Ending cash balance

Formula Best Practices

  1. Use descriptive sheet names: "Sales Budget" not "Sheet1"
  2. Organize logically: Tabs in budget flow order (Sales ‚Üí Production ‚Üí DM ‚Üí DL...)
  3. Use absolute references when copying formulas: $B$10
  4. Add comments to complex formulas: Insert ‚Üí New Comment
  5. Color-code: Input cells (yellow), formula cells (white), linked cells (blue)
  6. Lock assumptions: Protect worksheet with inputs visible
üí° TRACE DEPENDENCIES: Use Formulas ‚Üí Trace Precedents/Dependents to visualize links between sheets. This helps debug errors and understand the model structure!

Handling Circular References

Sometimes budgets create circular references (A depends on B, B depends on A):

‚ö†Ô∏è EXAMPLE: Interest expense depends on borrowing. Borrowing depends on cash needed. Cash needed depends on interest expense. Circular!

Solutions:

  • Enable iterative calculations: File ‚Üí Options ‚Üí Formulas ‚Üí Enable iterative calculation
  • Or manually calculate interest in next period (simpler)
  • Or use helper columns to break the circle

Testing Your Links

Verify all links work correctly:

  1. Change an assumption (e.g., January sales = 15,000 instead of 10,000)
  2. Check that all dependent cells update:
    • Production budget units
    • Materials purchases
    • Cash receipts
    • Income statement revenue
  3. Change assumption back to original value
  4. Verify everything returns to original amounts

Documentation

Create a "README" or "Assumptions" sheet that documents:

  • Where data comes from (sales forecast source, cost estimates)
  • Key assumptions (collection %, payment terms, inventory policy)
  • Sheet structure and flow
  • Contact person for questions
  • Last update date

Beginning vs. Ending Balances

Key to balance sheet construction:

Beginning Balance
+ Increases (purchases, sales, borrowing)
- Decreases (payments, collections, repayments)
= Ending Balance

‚öôÔ∏è Tutorial 6: Budget Assumptions & Best Practices

The quality of your master budget depends entirely on the quality of your assumptions.

Critical Assumptions to Document

1. Sales Assumptions

  • Unit sales forecast: Based on historical trends, market research, sales team input
  • Pricing strategy: Will prices change? Discounts? Promotions?
  • Seasonality: Are sales steady or does volume fluctuate?
  • New products: Launching anything that affects volume?
  • Competition: How might competitors affect our sales?

2. Production Assumptions

  • Inventory policy: How much buffer stock? (e.g., 20% of next month's sales)
  • Production capacity: Can we produce what we need? Any constraints?
  • Lead times: How long to produce a unit?
  • Efficiency: Expected yield, waste, rework rates

3. Cost Assumptions

Cost Type Key Assumptions
Direct Materials Price per unit, quantity per product, price changes, vendor reliability
Direct Labor Wage rates, hours per unit, overtime needs, raises/bonuses
Overhead Variable rate, fixed monthly costs, depreciation, utilities
S&A Commission rates, advertising spend, salaries, rent

4. Cash Flow Assumptions

Collection Pattern (Critical!):

When Collected % of Sales Rationale
Month of sale 70% Cash sales + quick-pay customers
Month after sale 28% 30-day credit terms
Uncollectible 2% Bad debts (expense but no cash)

Payment Pattern:

What When Paid
Materials 50% in purchase month, 50% next month
Labor 100% in month incurred
Overhead 100% in month incurred (minus depreciation)

Common Budgeting Pitfalls

‚ö†Ô∏è AVOID THESE MISTAKES:
  • Overly optimistic sales: "Hockey stick" growth rarely happens
  • Static costs: Forgetting that costs increase with volume/inflation
  • Ignoring timing: Sales ‚â† cash, expenses ‚â† payments
  • Forgetting one-time items: Equipment purchases, bonuses, taxes
  • No contingency: Build in buffers for uncertainty
  • Complexity overkill: Don't model to the penny‚Äîround to meaningful amounts

Best Practices for Budgeting

  1. Start with history: Use last year's actuals as baseline
  2. Adjust for known changes: Price increases, new products, market shifts
  3. Get input from all departments: Sales knows customers, production knows constraints
  4. Document everything: Why did you assume 10,000 units? Write it down!
  5. Build flexibility: Make it easy to update assumptions and recalculate
  6. Sensitivity analysis: What if sales are 10% lower? Higher?
  7. Review monthly: Don't create budget and forget it
  8. Revise when needed: If assumptions change materially, update the budget
üí° THE 80/20 RULE: Spend 80% of your time on the 20% of assumptions that matter most. Sales forecast and major costs are crucial. Don't agonize over whether office supplies will be $100 or $105 per month!

Contingency Planning

Always prepare alternative scenarios:

Scenario Assumption Changes Impact
Best Case Sales +15%, costs stable Strong profit, may need working capital
Most Likely Current plan Base budget
Worst Case Sales -15%, costs +5% Lower profit, may need financing

Communicating the Budget

Once complete, present the budget effectively:

  • Executive summary: 1-page overview of key numbers
  • Assumptions document: Detail all major assumptions
  • Variance analysis: Compare to last year, explain changes
  • Action items: What must happen to achieve the budget?
  • Risks: What could go wrong? Mitigation plans?
